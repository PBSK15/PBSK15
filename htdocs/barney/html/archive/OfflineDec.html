Holidays
This month, Barney Online celebrates holidays. It's the perfect time for young children to learn the importance of sharing, giving, and spending time with loved ones. Children will have fun celebrating the holidays with their friend, Barney.

In December, excitement is in the air. Families gather from near and far to celebrate together. It's a time for decorating, sharing, and spending time with loved ones. Including children in the fun will teach them valuable lessons like the importance of love, sharing and giving. Whatever holiday your family enjoys, below are some activities to celebrate them all!

Christmas
Christmas is celebrated on December 25. It celebrates the birth of Jesus Christ, whom Christians recognize as the Son of God.

Decorated trees
Make and decorate a miniature Christmas tree. Paint a pine cone with green tempera paint. When the paint has dried, decorate the tree by gluing on beads, ribbons, string or glitter. Use it as a holiday decoration.

Reindeer sandwiches
Cut a piece of sliced bread diagonally, forming two triangles. Help your child spread a thin layer of peanut butter on each triangle, using a plastic knife. Break curly pretzels so the pieces look like reindeer antlers. Stick two at the top corners of the triangle. Add raisins for eyes and a red cinnamon candy for the nose. Safety reminder: Very small children should not play with the raisins and cinnamon candy because of the possibility of choking. Make sure these two items are kept away from small children.

Hanukkah
Hanukkah begins this year on December 4 and lasts for eight days. On each of the eight nights, a candle is lit on a menorah. Hanukkah celebrates the inner spirit that led battles for religious freedom against impossible odds.

Menorah
Help your child trace both hands onto a piece of white construction paper. Cut out the hand shapes and glue them onto blue construction paper so that the shapes of the pinkies overlap. Cut out yellow paper flames to put on top of each finger and thumb. Add glitter to the flames for an added spark!

Dreidel snacks
Insert one end of a small pretzel stick into the center of a large marshmallow. Spread icing onto the bottom of the marshmallow, and press the bottom of a candy kiss onto the icing.

Eight Days of Hanukkah
Read Eight Days of Hanukkah by Harriet Ziefert, Viking. This is a brightly illustrated story about a family celebrating Hanukkah. It presents the symbols of Hanukkah in a simple way.

Kwanzaa
The African-American holiday of Kwanzaa lasts for seven days from December 26 through January 1. On each of the seven days, a child lights a candle on the kinara (the special candle holder). Other symbols include a straw mat representing African-American history and ideas, the seven candles representing the seven principles of the holiday; unity, self-determination, working together, sharing, purpose, creativity, and faith. Ears of corn represent children--the future, and fruits and vegetables represent the harvest and hard work.

Corn prints
Corn is a symbol of Kwanzaa that celebrates children--the future. Cut an ear of corn in half. Help your child make corn prints by dipping the round part of the ear of corn into paint (red, green and black) making a pretty decoration.

Kwanzaa placemats
Make placemats by weaving red and green strips of paper into a piece of black construction paper. Cover the placemats with clear contact paper for durability. Celebrate with fruit and vegetables (symbolizing the first harvest).

Read about Kwanzaa
Kwanzaa by Ella Grier, Viking. This book follows a family as they celebrate Kwanzaa. At the top of each page the principle and symbol for the day is mentioned. Recipes follow the text. You may want to read a more in-depth book about the holiday called K Is For Kwanzaa by Juwanda G. Ford, Scholastic. This book details information about Kwanzaa in an ABC format.

These activities can be enjoyed by all families:

Winter walk
Bundle up in warm clothes on a cold winter day and go for a walk. Blow in the cold air and see your breath, feel the cold wind, notice the barren trees. What other observations can your child make? Look for animal tracks in the snow. How do animals stay warm in the winter? How do people stay warm in the winter?

Holiday countdown
Make a calendar for the month of December. Circle the date of your holiday celebration. Begin a countdown by crossing off the days until the special day! Waiting won't seem so long with this holiday countdown.

Have a card writing party
Put on holiday music, and help your child make holiday cards using construction paper, markers and crayons. Everyone will have fun making cards, writing notes, coloring pictures, decorating envelopes and sticking on stamps. When finished, take your child to the post office to mail the holiday greetings.

Photo album
Look through old photo albums for pictures of your family celebrating a special holiday. Talk about family traditions and the people who attended the celebration. Discuss favorite holiday memories.

Barney Says: Talk with your child about holidays and family traditions.

  • What is your favorite holiday? What makes that holiday so special?
  • What is your favorite holiday food?
  • What do you like best about winter?

Good books to read: Cuddle up with your child and read a good book.

A Chair For My Mother by Vera Williams, Greenwillow. Children enjoy learning about giving and sharing in this warm story about family.

Barney's Christmas Surprise by Mark Bernthal, Lyrick Publishing. Young children experience the winter holiday season and the tradition of gift-giving.

Barney's Christmas Wishes by Stephen White, Lyrick Publishing. In this heartwarming story, BJ and Baby Bop experience the joy that giving is as rewarding as receiving.

The Snowman by Raymond Briggs, Random House. This wordless book is about the adventures of a little boy and the snowman he builds.

The Twelve Days of Christmas by Guy Davis and Gayla Amaral, Lyrick Publishing. Countdown the days until Christmas with Barney.
