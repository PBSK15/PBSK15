Cold Weather Warnings
by Reuben Reiman, M.D., F.A.A.P.


Does it ever feel like your child caught a cold last September that lasted until the first warm breeze of May? Although the symptoms may seem like one continuous sneezing fit, there are actually more than 200 rhinoviruses that can cause colds. And young children are more vulnerable than adults, since immunity to cold viruses increases with age.

Colds often appear within one or two days of exposure to a rhinovirus and last usually three to four days. Antibiotics are of no help because they are not successful against viruses. Coughs, which often accompany colds, are the body's method of expelling excess mucus from the lungs. A cough may linger or even intensify after the cold symptoms have begun to diminish. Unfortunately, too much coughing can irritate the lung passages and lead to more coughing. Check with your doctor if a cough is keeping your child up at night or if it lasts more than two weeks.

Although it's unrealistic to expect that you can protect your child from all colds, there are some ways to decrease their frequency and duration.

  • Avoid closed, heated rooms. Cold viruses flourish in warm, dry places, and the chances of transmission are higher because the germs can't escape the environment.
  • Stay away from people with obvious colds. This is not easy for kids in school or daycare.
  • Cover mouth or nose when coughing or sneezing. Teach your children how to do this.
  • Dispose of tissues properly after each use. Do not save or reuse them.
  • Wash hands more frequently during the "cold season." Cold viruses are easily transmitted when children touch an infected child (or discarded tissue), then touch their own noses and mouths.
  • Avoid smoke. Smoking reduces resistance by paralyzing the hairs in the nose that act as the first line of defense against infection.
  • Keep surfaces clean. A cold virus can live for several hours on countertops, toys, and telephones. A wipe with a disinfectant kills germs.
  • Where possible, limit stress in your family's life. Research has shown that people with high stress levels are more likely to catch a cold.

Your family may not be cold-free this cold season, but these recommendations could help provide a healthier winter.
