April/May: Fun With Music

Singing with props
Choose one prop that can be used while singing a favorite song. For example, use an umbrella while singing a rainy day song, a small bucket or pail while singing "Jack and Jill," or hats, scarves, and other simple props to enhance any song.

"Miss Mary Mack"

This traditional street chant is fun to clap to while singing. Sit facing your child. Clap your hands once, slap your thighs once, clap your hands once, and pat each other's hands three times.

"Miss Mary Mack, Mack, Mack,
All dressed in black, black, black,
With silver buttons, buttons, buttons,
All down her back, back, back.

Well, she asked her mother, mother, mother,
For fifteen cents, cents, cents,
To see the elephants, elephants, elephants,
Jump the fence, fence, fence.

Well, they jumped so high, high, high,
They touched the sky, sky, sky,
And never came back, back, back,
Till the fourth of July, ly, ly!"
(traditional)

"John Jacob Jingleheimer Schmidt"

Sing this song softly until after the word "shout." Then sing the ending loudly. Another idea is to sing the song four times. The first time, sing it in a loud voice. The second time, sing it using the same volume that you use when you talk. The third time, sing it softly, and the last time, sing it using a whisper.

"John Jacob Jingleheimer Schmidt,
His name is my name too.
Whenever I go out,
The people always shout,
'There goes John Jacob Jingleheimer Schmidt!'
Da Da Da Da Da Da Da Da!"
(words and music traditional)

"B-I-N-G-O"

"Bingo" is a simple song that all family members can sing. To help your child learn the song, write the name BINGO across the top of a large sheet of art paper or poster board. Ask your child to draw a dog below. Point to the letters as you sing together.

"B-I-N-G-O, B-I-N-G-O, B-I-N-G-O
And Bingo was his name, oh!"

"Johnny Works With One Hammer"

Sing this fun song, substituting your child's name for Johnny.

"Johnny works with one hammer,
One hammer, one hammer,
Johnny works with one hammer,
Then he works with two."

Continue, starting each verse with the next number. Try these movements as you sing:

1st verse: move one hand up and down in a hammering motion.
2nd verse: move both hands up and down in a hammering motion.
3rd verse: move both hands and one leg up and down in a hammering motion.
4th verse: move both hands and both legs up and down in a hammering motion.
5th verse: move both hands, both legs, and head up and down in a hammering motion, ending with:

"Then he works no more!"
(words and music traditional)

Moving to music
Play various styles of music (classical, rock & roll, polka, jazz, reggae, country western) and have your child move to the beat of the music.

Ribbon dance
Give your child a long ribbon or scarf. Play different kinds of music that your child can dance and move to.

Kitchen band
There are many musical instruments available in your very own kitchen! Try these: tap a wooden spoon on pots, pans, or plastic bowls; shake a securely closed plastic container full of rice or beans; tap two spoons together to make an interesting noise; use old pot lids as cymbals. Experiment with rhythms (fast and slow) and with loud and soft sounds. Play some music and play along to the beat of the song.

Marching band
Play a rousing march. You and your child can take turns leading the parade, marching to the beat. Added props might include flags to wave, a baton and a toy drum.

Shaker
Decorate two paper plates with markers or crayons. Pour one-half cup dried beans or rice onto one of the plates. Staple or tape the other paper plate on top. Now you're ready to shake, shake, shake!

Guitar
To make a guitar, stretch two or three rubber bands lengthwise around an empty tissue box. Attach a paper towel tube to one end of the box for the neck. Strum the rubber bands and sing a favorite song.

Drum
Cover an oatmeal box or coffee can with construction paper and decorate. Use another piece of construction paper (attached with a rubber band) or the box lid as the drum head. Gently tap with fingers to the beat of the music.

Stone clappers
Look for stones that comfortably fit in your child's hands. Clap them together as you sing favorite songs or march in a pretend parade.

Kazoo
Cover one end of a toilet paper tube with a small sheet of wax paper and secure it with a rubber band. Hum into the open end. Young children may need help learning to hum!

Barney Says: When you spend time talking to your child, it shows that you care.

  • What songs do you like to sing?
  • Would you like to learn to play a musical instrument someday?
  • What instrument would you like to play?

Good Books to Read Together: Making reading fun can help develop a lifelong love of reading.

Barney & Baby Bop's Band by Mark Bernthal, Barney Publishing. Baby Bop learns it is more fun to share.

Barney Works With One Hammer by Margie Larsen, Barney Publishing. This delightful tale utilizes music to help children learn to count from one to five.

Ben's Trumpet by Rachel Isadora, Greenwillow. Ben wants to play a trumpet, but he doesn't have one-until a musician discovers his dream.

BJ Makes Music by Margie Larsen and Mary Ann Dudko, Barney Publishing. BJ has fun with many different instruments.

Hand Rhymes by Marc Brown, Dutton. Hand games shown in full-color illustrations invite participation by young children.

Max the Music Maker by Miriam Stecher, Lothrop. Max finds music everywhere, even in the instruments he makes.

Music, Music for Everyone by Vera B. Williams, Greenwillow. Rosa plays the accordion and earns money to help her family while her grandmother is sick.

The Philharmonic Gets Dressed bby Karla Kuskin, Harper. The 105 member of the orchestra get ready for a performance.
